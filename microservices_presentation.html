The Hard Part
Challenges of Microservices
Moving from a centralized system to a highly distributed one introduces a new class of problems. Predictability disappears. Networks lie.
01
Distributed System Complexity
Centralized = predictable behavior, success, performance.
Distributed = unpredictable — networks fail, latency spikes, packets get lost. Every assumption breaks.
02
Testing — Who Owns Integration?
Unit testing one service is easy. But integration tests cross service boundaries. Who writes them? Who runs them? Who owns the failures? Time-consuming and often ungoverned.
03
Troubleshooting & Debugging
A request hops across 5 services. One returns an error. Which service caused it? Distributed tracing, correlation IDs, and centralized logging become survival tools — not nice-to-haves.
04
Responsibilities & Coupling Risk
If every change your team makes requires another team in a meeting — you've rebuilt a monolith in disguise. The result: Distributed Monolith — a "Big Ball of Mud" at scale.

The Distributed Monolith Warning
⚠️ How good intentions create a Big Ball of Mud
Many companies split their monolith into services but kept shared databases, tightly coupled APIs, and mandatory cross-team sync meetings for every release. The result is the worst of both worlds: distributed complexity WITH monolith coupling. The industry learned this the hard way — which is why we now have established principles and best practices for a successful microservices migration.
Where To Draw The Lines
Microservice Boundaries & Core Principles
Three failed attempts teach us the three rules that actually work.
❌ Why splitting by application layer fails
Every new feature touches Presentation, Application, and Data layers simultaneously. You need to coordinate across all three "microservices" for every single change — exactly the same problem as the monolith.

→ This violates Cohesion: things that change together must stay together. The logic that changes should live within a single service boundary.
❌ Why splitting by technology fails
Stakeholders don't know which team to approach for a business feature. The business/logic layer becomes a dumping ground because technology alone can't cleanly carve business concerns.

→ This violates Single Responsibility: every service should do exactly one business thing. Bounded to a given context.
❌ Why minimizing size (micro = tiny) fails
Splitting into too-tiny services creates a web of dependencies. Services call each other constantly — this is tight coupling distributed across the network. Now you've just made a monolith that's also slow and unreliable.

→ Size doesn't matter. "Micro" means small responsibility, not small code.
🔗
Cohesion
Elements that change together stay together. The logic for a capability lives inside one service boundary — never split across services.
🎯
Single Responsibility
Each service does exactly one thing. Creates clear APIs and is bounded to a specific business context. Easy to reason about, replace, or scale.
🔓
Loose Coupling
Little or no dependency on other services. Minimum inter-service communication. The size of a service is irrelevant — the coupling is what counts.
✅ The Golden Rule
A service is correctly sized when it is Cohesive + Single Responsibility + Loosely Coupled. "Micro" does not mean tiny. A service that satisfies all three principles is the right size, whatever that size may be.
Decomposition by Business Capabilities
Split services along what the business does. Each service maps 1:1 to a business capability: Order Management, Customer Accounts, Payments, Inventory.

Stable — capabilities rarely change. Clear ownership by business unit.
Decomposition by Domain (DDD)
Split along domain model boundaries: Core domain (your competitive edge), Supporting domains (enable the core), Generic domains (commodity, buy not build).

Right-sized services. Engineering clarity.

Migrating to Microservices
Where you start matters more than how fast you go. Choose the incremental path — every time.

Stop everything. Rebuild it all.

Impossible to estimate full effort upfront

Tips for a Smooth Migration
🧪
Add test coverage first. Before extracting any component, ensure it has solid unit and integration tests. You need a safety net.
📐
Define the component API explicitly. Treat the component as if it were already a separate service. Document its interface before you move it.
✂️
Isolate before extracting. Remove interdependencies between the candidate component and the rest of the monolith first. Don't extract a tangled mess.
🔒
Keep the same tech stack. Changing language or framework at the same time as extracting a service doubles your risk. Do one hard thing at a time.
